# Journal of Biogeography revision plan

## Central question

Why does intraspecific flower-colour variation occur as local morph coexistence within populations in some species, but as geographically structured differentiation among populations in others?

## Novelty claim

This study does not claim that climate–flower-colour associations are new. Its contribution is to compare the spatial organization of intraspecific flower-colour variation across a global literature-derived sample, while exposing evidence quality and sampling-effort sensitivity.

## Terminology

Use **intraspecific flower-colour variation** as the umbrella concept.

Distinguish:

1. **within-population flower-colour polymorphism** — discrete colour morphs documented as coexisting locally;
2. **geographically structured flower-colour variation** — colour states or morph frequencies differentiated among populations.

Do not describe all among-population cases as flower-colour polymorphism without explicit local coexistence evidence.

## Claims that are currently supported

- Documented cases can be classified by the spatial organization of colour variation, although evidence quality is heterogeneous.
- In the baseline-unambiguous subset, geographically structured cases show a narrower realised species-level moisture niche than within-population cases.
- The direction is stable to leave-one-family-out analysis.
- The association weakens when less secure enrichment-derived classifications are included.
- Classification uncertainty is therefore part of the biological inference, not merely a data-cleaning detail.

## Claims to avoid

- Climate causes flower-colour differentiation.
- The sample represents all flowering plants or all flower-colour polymorphisms globally.
- Among-population cases are necessarily locally adapted.
- Species-level climatic niche breadth measures morph-specific niche differentiation.
- The study discovers the first relationship between flower colour and climate.

## Revision sequence

### 1. Conceptual reframing

- Replace FCP as the universal umbrella with intraspecific flower-colour variation.
- Define the two spatial organizations explicitly.
- Rewrite hypotheses around spatial organization rather than presence of colour polymorphism.

### 2. Evidence and estimand

- State that the sample is a global literature-derived comparative sample of documented cases.
- Define the inferential population as documented cases, not all angiosperms.
- Separate baseline-unambiguous, enrichment-supported and unresolved evidence throughout.

### 3. Main result hierarchy

Primary result:

- baseline-unambiguous moisture-niche contrast, reported with effect size, uncertainty, permutation result and leave-one-family-out stability.

Secondary results:

- all-evidence estimate;
- spatial fragmentation and environmental-turnover analyses;
- evidence-source sensitivity.

Do not elevate isolated nominal p-values above the full robustness pattern.

### 4. Phylogenetic non-independence

Minimum acceptable package:

- family-clustered uncertainty;
- leave-one-family-out stability;
- family composition table;
- explicit limitation that family controls do not replace a full phylogenetic comparative model.

Add a phylogenetic model only if a defensible species tree and adequate coverage can be assembled without dominating the paper.

### 5. Journal-facing manuscript structure

1. Introduction: spatial organization of intraspecific trait variation as a biogeographic problem.
2. Methods: evidence assembly, spatial-scale classification, GBIF climate metrics and source-stratified sensitivity.
3. Results: sample composition, primary climatic result, robustness and evidence sensitivity.
4. Discussion: interpretation at species-niche level, alternatives, sampling bias and testable population-level predictions.

## Pre-submission reviewer checklist

- Are within-population polymorphism and geographic differentiation conceptually separated?
- Is every conclusion stated at the same biological level as the data?
- Is the literature-derived sampling frame explicit?
- Is the primary result identifiable without reading the supplement?
- Are classification uncertainty and sensitivity visible in the main text?
- Are causal and local-adaptation claims avoided?
- Is taxonomic non-independence addressed transparently?
