Dear Editors,

Please consider our manuscript, **“Moisture niche breadth and spatial organization of flower-colour variation,”** for publication as a Research Article in *Journal of Biogeography*.

Intraspecific flower-colour variation can be expressed as local coexistence of discrete variants or as geographic differentiation among populations. Although previous studies have related floral pigmentation to climatic and biotic gradients, comparative analyses have rarely treated this spatial organization of variation as a cross-species biogeographic property. Our manuscript addresses that distinction using a global, literature-derived dataset with source-level classification evidence, explicit evidence-quality sensitivity and reproducible occurrence-based climatic summaries.

The audited comparison contained 34 species from 25 families. Geographically structured rather than within-population variation was negatively associated with realised moisture niche breadth in the primary family-clustered model (odds ratio = 0.426, 95% confidence interval = 0.184–0.985; Wald p = 0.0460), although label-permutation support was borderline (p = 0.0556). A paginated, quality-filtered GBIF sensitivity analysis strengthened the estimate (odds ratio = 0.300, 95% confidence interval = 0.133–0.675; permutation p = 0.0164). The estimated direction also remained negative in Open Tree topology models and in fixed-seed, time-scaled V.PhyloMaker2 models, but all phylogenetic confidence intervals included one. We therefore present the relationship as exploratory and phylogenetically unresolved rather than confirmed or causal.

We believe the manuscript is suited to *Journal of Biogeography* because its central contribution is not another general correlation between flower colour and climate. It asks why documented intraspecific variation appears as local coexistence in some species and geographic differentiation in others, explicitly connects trait variation to spatial organization, and shows how literature-derived classification quality, occurrence sampling and phylogenetic treatment affect comparative inference. The workflow, frozen classification manifest, complete model matrix, search chronology and Supporting Tables S1–S17 make those uncertainties auditable.
